Designing for Ergonomics and Accessibility

Timeline: March 2022 - June 2022

Course: Design Thinking and Communication

Skills: Strategic Thinking, User-Centered Design

Collaborators: Computer Science Major (x3), Biomedical Engineering (x1)

Understanding the Problem: Ergonomics in Piano Playing

The project started with a critical question: How can we design a piano stand that supports long practice sessions without strain? To answer this, we sat down with a Northwestern professor to dive into their specific ergonomic needs. They described the discomfort of current piano stands on the market—too rigid, poorly adjusted for different seating positions, and ultimately a barrier to effortless playing. Listening to their experiences, we defined key design requirements :

    - Height Adjustability
    - Stability
    - Long-term Comfort

To ground our approach, we researched existing stands, analyzing where they fell short and identifying areas for innovation. We also measured a similar-sized table to approximate dimensions for prototypes (as we did not have direct access to the current stand). From our market research and user insights, we defined essential design requirements: height adjustability, stability, and long-term comfort.

Original piano stand [Left]. Measuring similar sized table for dimensions [Right].

From Ideas to Prototype: Hands-On Design Development

With clear user needs in mind, my team and I jumped into brainstorming sessions, sketching and evaluating multiple design ideas. Each concept had to strike a balance—functional yet simple, adjustable but sturdy. Our final design balanced comfort and practicality, incorporating adjustable legs for height flexibility, an integrated music stand, and cutouts for speakers and cables.

Initial Sketches of Our stand opened [Left] and closed [Right].

We translated our design requirements and sketches into a full-scale foam core prototype for user testing. To ensure accuracy, we also built a foam core mock piano keyboard, allowing for a more realistic evaluation. Through iterative testing, we refined the stand’s structure, optimizing stability, adjustability, and comfort.

Foam Core prototype open [Left] and closed [Right].

Construction & Testing: Bringing the Vision to Life

Once we finalized a direction, we began construction of the final prototype. Using hand tools and fabrication techniques, we cut, assembled, and reinforced the structure, focusing on stability and adjustability. Testing was an ongoing process—each version revealed new tweaks, whether in weight distribution, ease of height adjustment, or overall comfort. I documented key refinements along the way, ensuring we focused on our original goal: creating a stand that truly improved the playing experience.

Construction of Final Solution, body of stand [Left] and adjustable legs [Right].

Refining & Presenting the Solution

To prepare for the final presentation, we enhanced the prototype’s usability and aesthetics—painting the stand, fine-tuning its locking mechanism, and ensuring smooth adjustability. We then demonstrated the solution to our user, walking through its features and improvements. The professor’s feedback reinforced the impact of our design, validating that thoughtful, iterative problem-solving led to a stand tailored for comfort, stability, and ease of use.
