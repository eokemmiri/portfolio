From Internship Insights to User-Centered Solution

Timeline: June 2023 - August 2023

Course: CS 330 Human Computer Interaction

Skills: Product Design, User Experience Design

Collaborators: Completed Individually

Understanding the Problem: Friction in the Booking Journey

Booking a hotel should be seamless, yet user research showed travelers struggled with scattered information and excessive scrolling on a major hospitality brand’s details page. Drawing from my internship experience, I tackled this challenge for my Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) course final project. The goal: redesign the page to improve usability, streamline decision-making, and boost conversions while maintaining brand consistency.

Research & Insights: Understanding What Users Really Need

To build a design that truly met user expectations, I conducted a card-sorting exercise with 50 participants. Users categorized hotel details into three groups:

    - Essential for booking (e.g., hotel name, location, photos, check-in/check-out times).
    - Influences decision but not required (e.g., amenities, dining options, nearby attractions).
    - Not necessary for booking (e.g., meeting spaces, pet policies).

This research aligned with industry best practices used by leading competitors like Marriott, IHG, and Booking.com, which prioritize ease of navigation through structured content layouts. The results confirmed the need for a clear hierarchy, with critical information upfront and secondary details accessible through collapsible sections.

Card Sort Activity with all essential hotel details [unsorted-left, sorted-right].

Design & Implementation: Balancing Depth and Simplicity

Armed with user insights, I developed a redesigned interface that:

    - Reduced cognitive overload by grouping related information into intuitive categories.
    - Minimized scrolling through collapsible sections, allowing users to expand details only when needed.
    - Enhanced navigation with interactive filters, letting users tailor the page based on their priorities.

Throughout the process, I iterated on my design, continuously refining it based on feedback from usability testing. I designed a low fidelity prototype to map out the structure and user flow. This initial design focused on organizing information intuitively, reducing cognitive load, and improving navigability.

Low Fidelity Prototype of final design concept.

Reflections & Impact: Designing for Real World Usability

For the final design, I developed an interactive prototype that enhanced the brand's details page by emphasizing the most important booking information—hotel name, location, and photos. By introducing an adaptive content strategy, I ensured that general users could quickly access essential details, while niche travelers had easy access to additional options. This project deepened my expertise in user-centered design and data-driven decision-making, transforming the booking process into a more intuitive and efficient experience for all users.
