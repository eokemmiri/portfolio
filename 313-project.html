Introducing Young Learners To AI Through Hands-On Play

Timeline: March 2024 - June 2024

Course: CS 313 Tangible Interaction

Skills: Interactive Design, User-Centered Design

Collaborators: Computer Science Major (x2), Design Major

Problem & Exploration: Finding the Connection between AI literacy and Tangible Interaction

For this course project, my team explored tangible interaction—designing physical interactions with digital information—to create an innovative learning experience. Initially, we aimed to teach young learners about artificial neural networks (ANNs), which help computers recognize patterns and make decisions, similar to the human brain. We envisioned an interactive exhibit to break down complex AI concepts and demonstrate ANN functionality.

Through research and educator feedback, we refined our focus to foundational concepts. Logical reasoning and decision-making emerged as the ideal entry points—accessible for elementary students while building a strong foundation for AI literacy.

Initial Sketches of interactive exhibits with ANN as the learning objective.

Collaborative Design & Prototyping: Crafting an Engaging Educational Game

With this refined focus, we developed an engaging learning experience. Through educator feedback and early testing, we arrived at a Guess Who-style decision tree game, incorporating tactile elements to encourage exploration. As a team, we refined game mechanics and integrated sensor-based triggers for engagement. Leveraging my product management experience, I ensured alignment with learning objectives, reinforcing structured decision-making skills.

The final exhibit brought AI concepts to life through a decision tree where students physically navigated yes/no pathways to identify animals. Any incorrect answers led them to retrace steps, reinforcing logical reasoning. Players placed animal cards in the correct spots by answering simple questions like: Does the animal fly? or Does it live in water?

For our first testing session with elementary school students, we built the interface using durable cardboard and photoresistors to detect card placements. We observed that gameplay was shorter than expected and that added features would enhance engagement. Key challenges included troubleshooting sensors, refining interaction timing, and ensuring intuitive play. After team discussions, we incorporated audio cues for real-time feedback and expanded the animal set to increase complexity and engagement.

Initial Sketch of our final concept [Left]. First prototype at a user testing session of our Guess-Who game [Right].

Testing, Refinement, & Impact

We showcased the final prototype in two elementary school sessions, where students engaged with the decision-tree game using logical reasoning to identify animals. They collaborated by teaching peers how to play, learned from mistakes by retracing steps, and successfully found the correct answers. Incorporating feedback, we added more animals for complexity and audio cues for real-time guidance. Students found the game fun, expressed interest in purchasing it, and demonstrated a strong grasp of structured decision-making. Educator insights helped refine visibility, sensor feedback, and interaction flow, ensuring an intuitive and engaging learning experience.
